Estate planning for blended families.

Blended families are the planning scenario where 'standard' falls apart fastest. Here's what the default plan gets wrong, and what actually protects everyone.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 7 min read · Octus Financial

Blended families are the planning scenario where 'standard' falls apart fastest. A standard revocable trust leaves everything to a surviving spouse, then equally to children. In a first marriage with children from that marriage only, this works cleanly. In a blended family, it can produce outcomes that neither spouse intended and that no one realizes until it's too late to fix.

The default problem

Here's the scenario we see most often. A couple remarries later in life. Each brings children from a prior marriage. They have a combined estate with real property, retirement accounts, and investment accounts. They draft a trust that leaves everything to each other, then to 'our children' — with the assumption that the surviving spouse will ultimately leave assets to all the children equitably.

The first spouse dies. Everything passes to the survivor. The survivor, now alone, updates the estate plan. The new plan leaves everything to their own biological children. The stepchildren — who were named nowhere in a legally binding document — receive nothing.

This is not fraud. It is not a violation of the original trust. It is what happens when good intentions are not backed by enforceable structure.

The tools that actually protect everyone

A QTIP trust — Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust — is one of the most common solutions for blended families. When the first spouse dies, assets pass into the QTIP trust. The surviving spouse receives income from the trust for their lifetime and may have limited access to principal for health, education, maintenance, and support. When the surviving spouse dies, the remaining trust assets pass to the beneficiaries named by the first spouse — typically their biological children.

This structure provides for the surviving spouse without allowing them to redirect the ultimate inheritance. The first spouse's children are protected because their inheritance is locked in at the first death. A QTIP trust is irrevocable at the first death. That's what makes the protection real.

Separate trusts for separate assets

Another approach is keeping certain assets in separate trusts from the beginning. Pre-marital assets, an inheritance, or property you specifically want to pass to your biological children can be held in a separate trust that never becomes marital property. When clients come to us with a blended family situation, we ask early: which assets do you want to share, and which are specifically designated for your children from a prior relationship? The answer shapes the entire structure.

The conversation with your spouse

This planning requires an honest conversation that many couples defer. It requires acknowledging that your interests and your children's interests are not perfectly aligned, and that your spouse's interests and your stepchildren's interests may diverge after you're gone. That's not comfortable. But it's necessary. The alternative is a plan built on assumptions about what your surviving spouse will do — and assumptions are not estate planning.
