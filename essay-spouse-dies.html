What to do in the first 30 days after a spouse dies.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 8 min read · Octus Financial

This essay is written for the person who is in it right now, or who may be in it soon. It is practical by design. Grief doesn't need a framework — but the weeks after a death do. The first 30 days are not about optimizing. They're about not making mistakes that are hard to undo, taking the steps that have deadlines, and leaving everything else for later.

In the first 72 hours

Obtain certified death certificates. You will need more than you think. Financial institutions, insurance companies, government agencies, and real estate transactions all require original certified copies — not photocopies. Order at least 10 to 15. Your funeral home can usually order these as part of their services; ask immediately.

Locate the will and trust documents. Don't act on them yet — just find them. They may be in a home safe, a filing cabinet, a safe deposit box, or with the attorney who drafted them. If they're in a safe deposit box, some states restrict access after death; call the bank before you go.

Notify Social Security. If your spouse was receiving benefits, Social Security needs to be notified of the death, usually within the same month. Any payment made for the month of death or after must be returned. Call 1-800-772-1213.

In the first two weeks

File for survivor benefits if applicable. If your spouse was receiving a pension, annuity, or military benefit with a survivor option, notify the paying agency. If you are eligible for Social Security survivor benefits — generally available at age 60, or 50 if disabled — decide whether to file now or defer. This decision has long-term income implications worth discussing with an advisor.

Contact life insurance companies. You'll need a certified death certificate and the policy number for each policy. Ask about the payout options — lump sum, installments, or retained asset account — before you decide. You are not required to take a lump sum immediately.

Notify financial institutions. Banks, brokerages, and retirement account custodians all need to know. Bring a certified death certificate and, if assets were held in a trust, your Certificate of Trust.

In the first 30 days

Inventory the estate. List every asset you're aware of — real property, bank accounts, brokerage accounts, retirement accounts, vehicles, business interests, and personal property of significant value. This list becomes the foundation for everything that follows.

Identify accounts that may go through probate. If your spouse owned assets in their name alone — not jointly, not in trust, not with a named beneficiary — those assets may need to go through probate before they can be transferred to you. A local probate attorney can advise on your state's specific process and thresholds.

Don't make large financial decisions. This is the most important instruction on the page. The 30 days after a death are not the time to pay off the mortgage, restructure investments, make large gifts to family members, or change life insurance beneficiaries. Wait. Almost everything can wait.
