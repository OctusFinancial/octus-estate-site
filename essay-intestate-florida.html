What happens if you die without a will in Florida.

Dying without a will means Florida has already written your estate plan for you. The state's plan is logical in a general sense. It does not know your family, your relationships, or your intentions.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

Dying without a will is called dying intestate. In Florida, it means the state has already written your estate plan for you. The Florida Intestate Succession statutes determine who receives your assets, in what proportions, and in what order. The state's plan follows bloodlines. It tries to approximate what most people would want. It does not know your family, your relationships, or your intentions — and in many situations, it produces outcomes that would surprise the person who died.

What Florida's intestate rules actually say

If you die with a spouse and no descendants: your spouse inherits everything.

If you die with a spouse and descendants who are also the spouse's descendants: your spouse inherits everything.

If you die with a spouse and at least one descendant who is not your spouse's descendant — a child from a prior relationship: your spouse inherits half. Your descendants share the other half.

If you die with no spouse but with descendants: your descendants inherit everything, divided equally among your children, with a deceased child's share passing to their children.

If you die with no spouse and no descendants: assets pass to parents, then siblings, then more distant relatives. If you die with no living relatives at all: your estate escheats to the state of Florida.

Where the rules create problems

The blended family problem. If you are married and have children from a prior relationship, Florida's intestate rules split your estate between your spouse and your children — potentially giving your children a fractional interest in your home, your investment accounts, and everything else you own. Your spouse may be forced to share ownership of the family home with your adult children from a prior marriage. This is rarely what anyone intended.

The minor children problem. If your children are minors and inherit under intestate succession, the court will appoint a guardian of the property to manage their inheritance until they turn 18. At 18, they receive everything outright — no conditions, no trustee discretion, no ability to structure distributions for education or gradual access.

The unmarried partner problem. Florida intestate law does not recognize unmarried partners, regardless of how long you have been together. A partner of 20 years who is not a legal spouse inherits nothing under intestate succession. Everything passes to blood relatives.

The simultaneous death problem. If you and your spouse die in a common accident and it cannot be determined who died first, Florida's simultaneous death rules create a legal presumption that each predeceased the other for purposes of their own estate. Without a will or trust that addresses simultaneous death, the outcome may not resemble anything either person would have chosen.

What a will does that intestacy doesn't

A will names who you want to receive your assets. It names a personal representative you trust to handle the process. It can name a guardian for your minor children. It can create a testamentary trust with terms for how and when minor beneficiaries receive their inheritance.

A will does not avoid probate — it still goes through court. But it gives the court your instructions, rather than leaving the court to apply the state's default. A funded revocable trust does both: it gives the court your instructions and removes the assets from the probate process entirely.
