How to re-title a brokerage account, step by step.

This is the essay nobody writes because it isn't glamorous. But it may be the most practically useful thing on this page. You have a trust. You have a brokerage account. Here's exactly how to connect them.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 5 min read · Octus Financial

You have a trust. You have a brokerage account. The brokerage account needs to be re-titled into the trust's name. Here's exactly how to do it.

Step one: locate your Certificate of Trust

Your trust document is likely 30 to 60 pages long. No brokerage will read all of it. What they need is your Certificate of Trust — sometimes called an Abstract of Trust or Trust Certification. This is a 2-to-4-page summary that identifies the trust name, the date it was established, the trustee(s), the successor trustee(s), and the powers of the trustee. If you don't have one, your estate attorney can generate it.

Step two: call the right team

Don't use the general customer service line. Ask specifically for the team that handles trust account titling or account re-registration. At Fidelity, this is Estate Services. At Schwab, it's the estate and trust services team. At Vanguard, it's Account Maintenance.

Tell them: "I have an individual brokerage account and I need to re-title it into my revocable living trust. The trust uses my Social Security number. I have a Certificate of Trust. What is your process?"

Step three: complete their paperwork

Most brokerages will ask you to complete a re-registration form and provide your Certificate of Trust. Some require a notarized signature. Some accept a medallion stamp from a bank officer instead. Ask which they prefer before you start.

The new account title will read something like: Jane Smith and John Smith, Trustees of the Smith Family Trust, dated January 15, 2023. The exact format varies — let them tell you their preferred format and use it exactly.

Step four: confirm the re-titling

After the paperwork is processed — typically 5 to 10 business days — log in and verify the account title has changed. Download the updated account statement showing the new trust name and keep it with your estate planning documents.

What about retirement accounts?

Do not re-title retirement accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s) into your revocable trust. Doing so is treated as a taxable distribution — you would owe income tax on the entire balance immediately. Instead, review and update your beneficiary designations on retirement accounts. If you're considering naming your trust as a beneficiary on a retirement account, ask us first — it requires careful analysis to avoid adverse tax consequences.

What about the house?

Real property requires a new deed, not just a form. The deed needs to be prepared correctly for your state, signed, notarized, and recorded with your county clerk's office. Recording fees vary by county, typically $10 to $50. This is the one piece of funding we recommend having a professional prepare — a deed recorded with an error can create title complications.

Everything else — bank accounts, brokerage accounts, non-retirement investment accounts — you can generally handle yourself with a Certificate of Trust and a phone call.
