Funding

The unfunded trust epidemic — and how to spot one.

Roughly 60% of revocable trusts in the U.S. are never properly funded. A beautiful document sitting in a drawer doesn't control a single dollar. Here's how to check yours.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 7 min read · Octus Financial

There is a document sitting in a file drawer somewhere in roughly 60% of American households that have a revocable trust. It's a beautiful document. It might be 40 pages. It has the family name in the title. It was drafted by an attorney, signed in front of a notary, and filed with care.

It doesn't control a single dollar.

This is the unfunded trust — and it's the most expensive non-problem in estate planning. The family paid to create the trust. They believe the trust will protect them. When the grantor dies, the family discovers that the house, the brokerage account, and the savings account are all still titled in the deceased person's individual name. The trust can't do anything for those assets. Probate opens.

Why this happens

Funding a trust means re-titling assets so the trust owns them — or naming the trust as beneficiary on accounts that pass by designation. It requires actual paperwork, phone calls to financial institutions, and in the case of real property, a deed prepared and recorded with the county.

Most estate attorneys hand you the trust documents and a funding guide and consider the job done. The funding guide goes on the shelf. Life gets busy. Six months pass. Then six years. Then the grantor is gone and the trust never got funded.

How to check your own trust in 15 minutes

Pull out your trust document and locate the trust name — it reads something like The [Your Last Name] Family Trust, dated [date], [Your Name] as Trustee. That exact name needs to appear on your funded assets.

Brokerage accounts: Log in and look at how the account is titled. If it says your name alone — 'Jane Smith' — and not 'The Smith Family Trust, Jane Smith Trustee,' the account is not in the trust.

Bank accounts: Check the same way.

Your home: Pull up your county property appraiser's website and look up your address. The owner name listed is what's on the deed. If it's your personal name rather than the trust name, the property hasn't been deeded in.

Life insurance and retirement accounts: These generally should not be titled into a revocable trust (retirement accounts especially — doing so can trigger immediate taxation). Instead, check that your beneficiary designations are current and intentional.

What to do if you find a gap

For brokerage and bank accounts: call the institution and ask for their process to re-title an account into a trust. Most require a Certificate of Trust and a signature card.

For real property: you need a new deed prepared by someone who knows your state's requirements. A deed recorded incorrectly can create title problems down the road — worth paying a professional for.

For beneficiary designations: log into each account, navigate to beneficiary information, and review. Update anything that doesn't reflect your current intentions.
