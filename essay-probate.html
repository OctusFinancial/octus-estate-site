Probate: what it is, how long it takes, and why most families want to avoid it.

Most people know they want to avoid probate. Fewer know exactly what it is, why it takes as long as it does, or what avoiding it actually requires. Here's the plain version.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

Probate is a court-supervised legal process through which a deceased person's assets are identified, debts are paid, and remaining assets are distributed to heirs. It exists because without some mechanism for transferring ownership after death, there would be no orderly way to change title on a house, close a bank account, or transfer investments held in a deceased person's name.

The probate court validates the will (if there is one), appoints a personal representative to manage the estate, oversees the payment of debts and taxes, and ultimately authorizes distribution to beneficiaries. Every step requires court filings, notices, and waiting periods.

How long it actually takes

In Florida, a formal administration — the standard probate process for estates that don't qualify for simplified procedures — typically takes six months to a year for a straightforward estate. Complex estates, contested wills, or creditor disputes can extend that to two years or more.

Florida has a simplified process called Summary Administration for estates with non-exempt assets under $75,000, or where the decedent has been dead for more than two years. If your estate qualifies, this is faster. But most estates with a home, meaningful savings, or investment accounts will exceed that threshold.

There are mandatory waiting periods built into the process that cannot be shortened. Creditors have 90 days from the publication of a Notice to Creditors to file claims. That window has to close before distribution can proceed.

What it costs

Florida sets statutory attorney fees for probate, calculated as a percentage of the gross estate value — not the net. A $500,000 estate (including the full value of a home, regardless of the mortgage) produces statutory fees in the range of $15,000 to $20,000, split between the attorney and the personal representative. These fees can be higher if the estate is contested or complex. A funded trust eliminates this cost.

What probate reveals

Probate is a public process. The will, the inventory of assets, and the court filings are public record — accessible to anyone who searches the county clerk's database. For families who value privacy about their financial affairs, this matters. A funded trust keeps distribution private.

What avoiding probate actually requires

Assets avoid probate through one of three mechanisms: joint ownership with right of survivorship (the asset passes automatically to the surviving owner), beneficiary designation (retirement accounts, life insurance, payable-on-death bank accounts), or trust ownership (assets titled in the name of a funded revocable trust).

The mistake most families make is assuming that having a will is the same as avoiding probate. It isn't. A will is the document that goes through probate. It governs what happens to assets that are subject to probate — but it doesn't remove assets from that process. Only the three mechanisms above do that.
