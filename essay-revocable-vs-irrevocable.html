Revocable vs. irrevocable — and why most families want the revocable one.

The distinction sounds technical. The stakes are real. Here's what each type of trust actually does, who each one is for, and why the answer is almost always the same.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

The word irrevocable sounds serious. Final. Grown-up. It's the kind of word that makes people nod as if they already knew what it meant.

Most people don't. And the distinction matters more than almost anything else in estate planning.

Here's the short version: a revocable trust is one you can change, dissolve, add to, or take back at any point during your lifetime. You stay in control. You can move assets in and out. If your family situation changes — a divorce, a new grandchild, a falling-out with a beneficiary — you update the document and keep going.

An irrevocable trust is one you cannot easily change. Once assets go in, they are no longer legally yours. That's not a flaw — it's the feature. Irrevocable trusts exist primarily to accomplish things a revocable trust cannot: shielding assets from Medicaid spend-down, removing assets from a taxable estate above the exemption threshold, or protecting an inheritance from a beneficiary's creditors or ex-spouse.

So who actually needs an irrevocable trust?

Clients with very large estates — historically above the federal exemption, which has shifted significantly and is scheduled to decrease again in 2026. Clients with specific Medicaid planning needs. Clients who have a beneficiary with a disability and need a special needs trust structure. Business owners with complex asset protection goals.

That's a meaningful subset of the population. But it is not most families.

Most families need a revocable living trust. It avoids probate, keeps their affairs private, names a successor trustee who can step in if they become incapacitated, and makes sure assets pass to the right people without a court supervising the handoff.

It won't reduce your estate taxes if your estate is above the exemption threshold. It won't protect assets from nursing home costs. It's not designed to. But for the family with a home, retirement accounts, a brokerage account, and children they want to provide for — it does exactly what needs to be done.

The control question

What surprises most people when they learn this distinction: with a revocable trust, you are both the grantor and the trustee during your lifetime. You manage your assets exactly as you did before. The trust doesn't feel like a trust from the inside — it just quietly removes the probate step when you die.

Irrevocable trusts work differently. Depending on the structure, you may give up the ability to serve as trustee. You may not be able to access the principal. You are, by design, giving something up in exchange for a legal benefit.

Neither is better in the abstract. The question is what you need it to do.

How we think about this in meeting one

In our first meeting, we ask about your family structure, your assets, and what you're worried about. For most clients, the answer points squarely at a revocable trust — and we tell them so.

When the situation is more complex — a blended family, a beneficiary with a substance use disorder, an estate approaching the exemption threshold — we'll talk through whether additional structures make sense, and refer you to a specialist when that's what's warranted.

What we won't do is recommend an irrevocable trust because it sounds more thorough. That's not planning. That's just using a bigger word.
