POAs

Powers of attorney explained — financial, medical, and why you need both.

Powers of attorney are the documents people mean when they say they want someone to 'be able to take care of things' if they can't. There are two kinds. They do different things. You need both.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 7 min read · Octus Financial

Powers of attorney are the documents people mean when they say they want someone to 'be able to take care of things' if they can't. They are the most immediately useful documents in an estate plan — and the ones most commonly misunderstood. There are two kinds, and they do different things. You need both.

The financial power of attorney

A financial power of attorney — sometimes called a durable power of attorney — authorizes someone (your agent) to manage your financial affairs on your behalf. It can be broad or narrow, immediate or springing.

Broad authority covers banking, investment management, tax filings, real estate transactions, business operations, and virtually any other financial matter. For most individuals, a broad durable financial POA is the right choice. It gives your agent the flexibility to handle whatever comes up, rather than requiring you to anticipate every possible scenario in advance.

Durable means the POA remains effective if you become incapacitated — which is exactly when you need it. A non-durable POA terminates if you lose mental capacity. For estate planning purposes, durable is the default.

What a financial POA does not do

It terminates at death. Once you die, your personal representative or successor trustee takes over. The agent under a power of attorney has no authority after the principal dies.

It does not override a trust. If your assets are held in a revocable trust, your successor trustee manages those assets. The financial POA covers assets held in your personal name — it is a companion to the trust, not a replacement for it.

It does not authorize the agent to change your estate plan. Your agent can manage assets, but cannot change the beneficiaries of your accounts or the terms of your trust. Those require your signature while competent.

The healthcare power of attorney

A healthcare power of attorney — called a healthcare surrogate designation in Florida — names someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot make them yourself. This is a completely separate document and a separate role.

Your financial agent and your healthcare agent can be the same person. Often they are. But they don't have to be. The skill set for managing finances and the skill set for advocating in a medical crisis are different. Name who is actually best suited for each role.

Florida-specific requirements

Florida has specific execution requirements for both documents. A Florida durable power of attorney must be signed in the presence of two witnesses and a notary. A healthcare surrogate designation has its own signing requirements.

A POA drafted in another state may not be honored by Florida financial institutions or healthcare providers if it doesn't meet Florida's standards. If you have moved to Florida or own Florida property since your documents were drafted, have them reviewed against current Florida law.

The one thing people get wrong

They name someone because they feel they should, without having the conversation about what that role actually involves. Your financial agent may need to manage your affairs for months or years during incapacity. They should know they're named. They should understand what they're agreeing to. The document names them. The conversation makes it real.
