For New Parents

Naming a guardian for your children is the hardest 20 minutes.

Most couples can draft their entire estate plan with minimal disagreement. Then we get to guardian designation. This is the question that stalls more estate plans than any other.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

Most couples can draft their entire estate plan with minimal disagreement. Asset allocation, beneficiary order, trustee selection — these conversations usually go smoothly. Then we get to guardian designation. This is the question that stalls more estate plans than any other. Who raises your children if both parents die?

The framework for the conversation

Before you name anyone, answer these questions between the two of you — not in the meeting, before it.

Geography: where does this person live? Are you comfortable with your children moving there, potentially changing schools and communities, at the most disruptive moment of their lives?

Values: does this person share your approach to education, religion, discipline, and what a healthy childhood looks like? Not perfectly — but in the ways that matter to you.

Capacity: does this person have the physical energy, emotional bandwidth, and financial stability to parent additional children? A beloved aunt who is 72 and managing her own health concerns may not be the right answer, even if she's the person your children love most.

Willingness: have you asked? This is the question most families skip. Name someone who knows they're named, has agreed to it, and understands what they're agreeing to.

The question most couples avoid

Which set of grandparents gets named?

In most families, both sets of grandparents want the answer to be them. And so parents avoid naming anyone, waiting for a resolution that doesn't come, and the estate plan never gets done.

Here is the honest answer: grandparents, even wonderful ones, are often not the right long-term guardian choice because of age. If your children are young and your parents are in their 60s or 70s, the math is hard. A guardian who becomes unable to parent when your child is 12 creates a second disruption. That doesn't mean grandparents can't be involved. It means the guardian question and the 'who is most involved in my children's lives' question are different questions.

You can name a backup

Your trust and will should name both a primary guardian and a contingent guardian. If the primary is unable or unwilling to serve when the time comes, the contingent steps in. This also reduces the pressure on the primary decision. You don't have to get it perfectly right the first time. You update your documents as your family's circumstances change.

One more thing

The guardian you name raises your children. The trustee you name manages their inheritance. These do not have to be the same person — and for many families, they shouldn't be. The best caregiver and the best financial steward are often two different people. Name them both.
