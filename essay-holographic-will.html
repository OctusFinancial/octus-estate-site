Wills

Why a holographic will is a worse idea than you think.

In many states, a handwritten will is legally valid. That doesn't mean it's a good idea. Here's what can go wrong — and what to do instead.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 5 min read · Octus Financial

A holographic will is a will written entirely in your own handwriting and signed by you. No witnesses. No notary. Just a piece of paper. In many states, that's legally valid. And it's almost always a mistake.

Not because handwritten wills are inherently invalid — in the states that recognize them, they can hold up in court. But because the conditions under which people write them, and the problems they create when they're offered for probate, make them a poor substitute for a properly executed document.

Why people write them

Usually urgency or avoidance. Someone gets a serious diagnosis and wants to put something on paper before they can get to an attorney. Or someone has been meaning to do estate planning for years and settles for a handwritten document as a 'good enough for now' solution that becomes permanent by default. Both are understandable. Neither produces a document you'd want your family to rely on.

The problems they create

Proving handwriting. A holographic will offered to probate court has to be authenticated — the court needs to be convinced the handwriting is actually yours. This typically requires testimony from people who knew your handwriting well. If those people are unavailable, or disagree, the will can be contested.

Ambiguity. Formal wills are drafted to anticipate edge cases. What if a named beneficiary dies before you? Who has authority to sell property to settle the estate? A handwritten will drafted in an afternoon rarely addresses these questions, and the gaps get filled by a probate judge who didn't know you.

State recognition. Not all states recognize holographic wills. Florida does not. If you write one in a state that does and then move to Florida, its validity becomes uncertain.

The DIY instinct applied to the wrong document. A handwritten note about where you keep your passwords is genuinely useful. A handwritten document disposing of your house, your retirement accounts, and your personal belongings is not the place to improvise.

What to do instead

If you have nothing and need something now, a holographic will in a state that recognizes it is better than dying intestate. That's a real floor, not a recommendation.

If you have any assets of consequence, any children, or any complexity in your family situation, a properly witnessed and notarized will — drafted with guidance and state-specific provisions — is not a luxury. It's the document. Florida requires two witnesses and a notary for a valid will. Handwritten documents don't meet that standard.

The gap between 'nothing' and 'something scrawled on a legal pad' is smaller than people assume. An Octus Complete Plan takes three meetings over about three weeks. The holographic will sits in a drawer and creates problems. A real will sits in a binder and solves them.
