Revocable trust, or a will? Sometimes both.

A will alone passes your assets through probate. A revocable trust avoids probate, but only works for what you've actually titled into it. Here's what each does, and when each one is enough.

Recommended for most

Revocable Living Trust

A legal arrangement that holds your assets during your lifetime, lets you stay in full control, and transfers them privately — without court — when you die or become incapacitated.

ProbateAvoids probate entirely for funded assets
PrivacyPrivate. Never becomes a public court record
IncapacitySuccessor trustee can manage assets immediately
Speed of transferDays to weeks after death
Out-of-state propertyAvoids ancillary probate in each state
Cost upfrontHigher — drafting and funding required
Cost at deathMinimal — no probate fees
MaintenanceRequires keeping assets titled in the trust
Right in some cases

Last Will & Testament

A legal document that directs how your assets are distributed at death and names guardians for minor children. It only takes effect after death — and only through probate court.

ProbateRequired. Court-supervised process
PrivacyPublic record. Anyone can read it
IncapacityNo effect — wills only act at death
Speed of transferTypically 6–18 months
Out-of-state propertyRequires separate probate in each state
Cost upfrontLower — simpler document
Cost at deathProbate fees, often 3–7% of estate
MaintenanceMinimal — update on major life events

Which is right for you?

A short rubric.

These aren't rules — they're patterns. The discovery meeting is where we get specific to your situation.

A trust is usually right when…

  • You own real estate, especially in more than one state.
  • Your estate is worth over ~$200,000 in non-retirement assets.
  • You want privacy — to keep your affairs out of public probate records.
  • You have minor children or beneficiaries with special needs.
  • You want a clear plan for incapacity, not just death.
  • You'd like assets to transfer quickly and without court oversight.

A will + POAs may be enough when…

  • You're young, renting, and your main asset is a 401(k) with named beneficiaries.
  • You have minor children — and need to name guardians — but limited assets.
  • Your state has a streamlined small-estate probate process.
  • You want the lowest upfront cost and can revisit as life evolves.
The most-skipped step

A trust only works if it's funded.

An unfunded trust is a piece of paper. Funding means re-titling your house, your accounts, and your investments into the trust — and updating beneficiaries to match. This is the step most planners hand to you as homework.

At Octus, we sit with you in meeting three and complete the funding paperwork together. You leave with a plan that's live, not pending.

A funded trust includes

  • ✓ Real estate — deed transferred to the trust
  • ✓ Bank accounts — re-titled to the trust
  • ✓ Brokerage & investments — transferred or pay-on-death
  • ✓ Business interests — assigned to the trust
  • ✓ Beneficiary updates — retirement accounts & insurance
