The difference between a regular deed and a Lady Bird deed — and why Florida families should know.

Most people have never heard of a Lady Bird deed. For Florida homeowners, it may be one of the most useful tools in estate planning — or it may be the wrong choice entirely. Here's how to tell.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 7 min read · Octus Financial

Most people have never heard of a Lady Bird deed. It has an unusual name (more on that in a moment), it's only recognized in a handful of states, and it solves a specific problem elegantly enough that it's worth understanding — especially if you own a home in Florida.

What a regular deed does

When you own real estate, your ownership is established by a deed — a legal document that records who holds title to the property. When you die, that title needs to transfer somehow. In the absence of any planning, a property held in your name alone goes through probate, where a court supervises the transfer to your heirs.

There are ways to avoid this. One is titling the property jointly with right of survivorship — the property passes automatically to the surviving co-owner when one dies. Another is deeding the property into a revocable trust — the trustee manages it, and on your death the successor trustee transfers it to beneficiaries without court involvement.

A third option, available in Florida, is the enhanced life estate deed — commonly called the Lady Bird deed.

What a Lady Bird deed does

A Lady Bird deed transfers property to named beneficiaries at your death — automatically, without probate — while allowing you to retain complete control during your lifetime. You can sell the property, mortgage it, rent it, renovate it, or revoke the deed entirely without the beneficiary's consent or knowledge. The beneficiaries have no present ownership interest. They receive the property only upon your death, and only if you haven't done something else with it first.

This is the key distinction from a regular life estate deed. In a traditional life estate, you transfer a remainder interest to beneficiaries at the time of signing. From that point forward, you cannot sell or mortgage the property without their consent — because they already have a legal ownership interest. A Lady Bird deed preserves your full control. The beneficiaries' interest is contingent, not vested.

Why it's called a Lady Bird deed

The story goes that a Florida attorney named Shapiro used President Lyndon Johnson and his wife Lady Bird as example names when drafting an early version of this deed type in the 1980s. The nickname stuck. The official legal term is 'enhanced life estate deed.'

Where it works well

A Lady Bird deed is often a good fit for a single homeowner — or a married couple — who wants to avoid probate on their primary residence without going through the full process of establishing and funding a revocable trust. The deed is simpler to execute and less expensive to prepare than a trust. For a family whose only significant non-retirement asset is the family home, it can accomplish the probate-avoidance goal cleanly.

It is also commonly used in Medicaid planning. Because you retain the right to revoke the deed and the property doesn't pass to beneficiaries until death, Florida does not count a Lady Bird deed transfer as a disqualifying gift for Medicaid purposes. This means the property can transfer to your heirs at death without going through probate — and without triggering Medicaid's estate recovery program to the same extent a regular deed transfer might.

Where it's the wrong choice

A Lady Bird deed only handles the property named in the deed. It does nothing for your bank accounts, brokerage accounts, retirement accounts, vehicles, or any other asset. If your goal is a comprehensive plan that handles your entire estate and provides for incapacity, a funded revocable trust does far more.

It also doesn't provide trustee-level control over how and when beneficiaries receive the property. If your beneficiaries are minors, or if you want to create conditions around the inheritance, a trust is the right tool.

And if you own property in multiple states, a Lady Bird deed only works in the states that recognize it — Florida is one of them, but many states are not. Out-of-state property needs its own solution.

Lady Bird deed vs. trust — which one?

For a Florida homeowner with a straightforward situation — no minor children, no complex family dynamics, a single property — a Lady Bird deed may be sufficient for the home, paired with current beneficiary designations on financial accounts. This is a lower-cost starting point that achieves meaningful probate avoidance.

For a Florida homeowner with a more complex situation — multiple properties, minor children, a blended family, a desire for incapacity planning, or assets that need trustee oversight — a revocable trust is the more complete solution. The Lady Bird deed can still be used for the Florida home, as one component of a broader plan.

The honest answer is that these tools aren't competing. They solve different problems. What matters is understanding which problem you're actually trying to solve.
