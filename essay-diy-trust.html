Why a "DIY trust" almost never survives first contact with reality.

Online estate planning platforms have gotten very good at the beginning of the process. Here are the four edge cases where things go quietly wrong — and stay wrong until your family finds out.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 7 min read · Octus Financial

Online estate planning platforms have gotten very good at the beginning of the process.

The interface is clean. The questions feel thorough. You finish in an afternoon, pay $299, and receive a PDF. It feels like the thing is done. It usually isn't. And the four edge cases below are where things go wrong — quietly, with no notification, until the people you were trying to protect find out the hard way.

Edge case one: the trust was never funded

This is the most common failure, and the one online platforms handle worst. A revocable trust only controls the assets that have been legally transferred into it — retitled in the name of the trust, with the trust listed as owner or beneficiary.

The platform gives you the document. It doesn't sit with you while you call your brokerage, walk you through the letter to send to your bank, or help you deed your home into the trust. That part gets handed to you as a checklist. Checklists have a completion rate. For trust funding, that rate is low. Roughly 60% of trusts in the U.S. are never fully funded — which means when the grantor dies, the assets still pass through probate. The exact outcome the trust was designed to prevent.

Edge case two: successor trustee provisions that don't hold up

Online forms ask you to name a successor trustee. They don't ask how your relationship with that person actually works, whether they live in the same state, whether they have the temperament or organizational capacity to manage assets during a grief-filled transition, or whether naming them will create conflict with your other beneficiaries.

They also don't help you name a backup. If your primary successor trustee dies before you or becomes incapacitated, and you haven't named a contingent — a court will appoint someone. Which is the outcome you were trying to avoid.

Edge case three: beneficiary designations that contradict the trust

Your trust doesn't automatically control your retirement accounts, your life insurance, or any asset that passes by beneficiary designation. Those accounts go directly to whoever is named — regardless of what your trust says.

We see this frequently: a trust drafted to leave everything equally to three children, and a 401(k) with only one child named as beneficiary because the account was opened fifteen years ago and never updated. The trust says one thing. The beneficiary designation says another. The beneficiary designation wins.

Edge case four: the state-specific provisions that didn't get included

Estate law is state law. Homestead exemptions, spousal rights, Medicaid rules, deed requirements — all of these vary. A generic online form built to work in all 50 states is, in practice, optimized for none of them. Florida, for example, has specific homestead restrictions that affect how a primary residence can be left to non-spouse beneficiaries. A trust that doesn't account for these specifics may not accomplish what the grantor intended.

What this is not

It's not an argument that everyone needs to spend thousands of dollars on estate planning. The $299 will is infinitely better than no document at all. It's an argument for knowing what you're getting. A DIY trust gives you a document. A guided process gives you a trust that actually works — funded, state-specific, with provisions that will hold up when they're tested.
