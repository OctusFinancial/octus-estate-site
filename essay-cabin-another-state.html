Multi-State

If you own a cabin in another state, read this first.

The vacation property is, by a significant margin, the asset most likely to cause complications in an otherwise straightforward estate. Here's why — and the fix is simpler than you'd expect.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

The vacation property is, by a significant margin, the asset most likely to cause complications in an otherwise straightforward estate. Not because it's complicated in isolation. But because real property is governed by the laws of the state in which it sits — and when you die owning property in a state where you don't live, that state's courts may get involved.

What ancillary probate is

Probate is the court-supervised process by which a deceased person's assets are transferred to their heirs. When that process happens in a state other than the one where you lived, it's called ancillary probate.

If you live in Florida and own a cabin in North Carolina, your Florida estate may pass through Florida probate. Your North Carolina cabin may additionally require a separate ancillary probate proceeding in North Carolina — with North Carolina attorneys, North Carolina court fees, and North Carolina timelines. Ancillary probate is not catastrophic. But it is avoidable. And it adds cost, delay, and complexity to what your family has to manage at an already difficult time.

The solution is simpler than people assume

If you have a properly funded revocable trust, real property titled in the trust's name avoids both primary and ancillary probate. The successor trustee simply administers the trust — no court involvement required, in either state.

The key word is funded. The cabin has to be deeded into the trust. Not just mentioned in the trust document — actually re-titled via a new deed, recorded in the county where the property sits. This requires a deed prepared under the laws of the state where the property is located. A deed prepared in Florida format may not record correctly in North Carolina.

Other things that vary by state

Powers of attorney: some states require state-specific POA language or formatting. An agent trying to use a Florida POA in Montana may encounter resistance from financial institutions that aren't familiar with Florida's statutory form.

Spousal rights: community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin) treat marital assets differently than common law states. If you acquired property while living in a community property state and then moved, those assets may carry community property characteristics with them.

Homestead protections: Florida's homestead laws significantly restrict how a primary residence can be devised if the owner is survived by a spouse or minor children. An out-of-state trust drafted without these provisions in mind may inadvertently conflict with Florida law.

What to do

Identify every state in which you own real property. When you work with Octus, we ask this question in meeting one. Every property in every state needs to be accounted for in your funding plan. For out-of-state property, your estate plan should include a deed prepared under that state's requirements. We coordinate this through our attorney network; you won't need to find a separate attorney in each state.
