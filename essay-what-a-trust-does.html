What a revocable trust actually does — and what it doesn't.

The revocable living trust is the foundation of most estate plans we put together. It's also one of the most misunderstood documents in personal finance. Here's the plain version.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

The revocable living trust is the foundation of most estate plans we put together. It is also one of the most misunderstood documents in personal finance — oversold in some corners, dismissed in others, and rarely explained in plain terms.

What a revocable trust does

It avoids probate. This is its primary job. When you die, assets held in your trust transfer directly to your beneficiaries — without a court supervising the process, without public filings, and without the delays that probate typically adds. In Florida, probate can take anywhere from several months to more than a year depending on complexity. A funded trust sidesteps that entirely.

It keeps your affairs private. Probate is a public proceeding. The inventory of your estate, the names of your beneficiaries, and the distribution of your assets become part of the court record — searchable by anyone. A trust transfers assets privately.

It provides for incapacity. A revocable trust names a successor trustee who can step in and manage trust assets if you become unable to manage them yourself. If you are incapacitated and your assets are in your trust, your successor trustee can pay bills, manage investments, and handle your financial affairs without going to court for a guardianship.

It gives you control over how and when beneficiaries receive assets. A simple will says 'I leave everything to my children.' A trust can say something more nuanced — your children receive their share at age 25, or earlier for education expenses, with the trustee having discretion for health and emergency needs. For families with minor children or beneficiaries who may not be ready to manage a lump sum, this is a meaningful difference.

It handles multiple states. If you own real property in more than one state, a funded trust means your family avoids probate proceedings in each state. Without a trust, each state's court may get involved separately.

What a revocable trust does not do

It does not reduce your income taxes. Assets in a revocable trust are still your assets for tax purposes. The trust uses your Social Security number. Income generated by trust assets is reported on your personal return. Nothing changes there.

It does not protect assets from creditors during your lifetime. Because you retain full control — you can revoke the trust, take assets out, change the terms — creditors can still reach those assets. Asset protection requires a different structure, usually irrevocable.

It does not automatically reduce estate taxes. A revocable trust does not move assets out of your taxable estate. For families with estates well below the federal exemption threshold, this doesn't matter. For estates approaching or above the threshold, additional planning is needed.

It does not control assets that aren't in it. This is the most important limitation. A trust only governs what has been transferred into it. Your retirement accounts, life insurance, and any asset with a named beneficiary pass outside the trust entirely — directly to whoever is named. Any asset still in your individual name at death may still go through probate, regardless of what your trust says.

The question worth asking

When someone asks whether they need a trust, the answer usually comes down to three things: do you own real property, do you want privacy, and do you want to avoid the cost and delay of probate for your family? If yes to most of those — a trust is probably the right foundation.

If your estate is straightforward, you have no real property, and your assets all pass by beneficiary designation or joint ownership, a well-drafted will with current beneficiary designations may be sufficient. We'll tell you which applies to your situation in meeting one.
