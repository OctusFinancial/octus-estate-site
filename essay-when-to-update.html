Life Events

When to update your estate plan.

Estate plans don't expire. But they do go stale. A plan that was right for your life five years ago may not reflect your life today. Here are the events that should trigger a review.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

Estate plans don't expire. But they do go stale. And a plan that was right for your life five years ago may not reflect your life today — or the people you want to protect. Most financial professionals recommend reviewing your estate plan every three to five years. That's reasonable as a floor. Certain life events should trigger a review immediately.

Marriage

If you married after your estate plan was drafted, your spouse may not be adequately provided for — particularly if your documents predate the marriage. Florida law provides some automatic protections for surviving spouses, but those statutory rights are not a substitute for intentional planning. Update your trust, will, and beneficiary designations to reflect your current family.

If this is a second marriage, the update conversation is more nuanced. Assets you want to leave to children from a prior relationship need to be specifically structured to survive the marriage. Leaving everything to a new spouse and hoping it finds its way to your children later is not a plan.

Divorce

In Florida, divorce automatically revokes any bequests to a former spouse in your will and removes them as a beneficiary of your revocable trust. But it does not automatically update your beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, or payable-on-death accounts. Those require affirmative action. We have seen cases where a person died years after a divorce, and an ex-spouse received the entire IRA because no one updated the beneficiary form. The will said otherwise. The beneficiary designation controlled. After a divorce, update every beneficiary designation on every account.

Birth or adoption of a child

If you have children born or adopted after your trust and will were drafted, review your documents to confirm they are included. Some trusts are written broadly enough to capture 'all children then living or thereafter born' — but not all. And the guardian designation should be reviewed any time your family grows.

Death of a named person in your documents

If a beneficiary, trustee, successor trustee, personal representative, or guardian you named has died, your plan has a gap. Who steps in for that person? Review your documents any time someone named in them dies.

Moving to a new state

Estate law is state law. A plan drafted in Virginia has different provisions than one drafted in Florida. Homestead, community property characterization, POA language, and healthcare directive requirements vary enough between states to matter. If you have moved to Florida, have your documents reviewed against current Florida law.

Change in your relationship with a named beneficiary

Estrangements happen. Addictions develop. A beneficiary's circumstances change in ways that make an outright inheritance a poor idea. If your current documents would leave a significant inheritance to someone you are no longer certain about, that's a review. You don't have to disinherit anyone — but you can restructure distributions to reflect your actual intentions.

The practical answer

Review your estate plan after every major life event. Read through your documents once every three years regardless. Make sure every beneficiary designation on every account reflects your current intentions. Estate plans are living instructions for the people you love. Keep them current.
