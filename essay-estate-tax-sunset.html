The 2026 estate tax sunset, in plain English.

Most people believe the estate tax applies to them. For most people, it doesn't — yet. Here's what's actually changing in 2026, who it affects, and what to actually do about it.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

The estate tax is one of the most politicized and misunderstood areas of personal finance. Most people believe it applies to them. For most people, it doesn't — yet. That may change in 2026. Here's what's actually happening, in plain language.

The current exemption

The federal estate tax only applies to estates above the exemption threshold. In 2024, that threshold is $13.61 million per individual — $27.22 million for a married couple using portability. Everything below that threshold passes to heirs free of federal estate tax. By most estimates, fewer than 1% of American estates owe any federal estate tax at current exemption levels. If your estate is below $13.61 million, the federal estate tax is not your current problem.

What happens in 2026

The elevated exemption was created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017. It is scheduled to sunset on December 31, 2025, reverting to pre-TCJA levels — roughly $5 to $7 million per individual, adjusted for inflation. The exact number won't be known until the IRS publishes it.

That's still a high threshold for most families. But it brings a meaningful number of estates — primarily those with significant real estate, business interests, or investment portfolios — from 'not affected' to 'affected.' Congress may act to extend the current exemption, reduce it further, or something else entirely. As of this writing, no legislation has passed. Planning under uncertainty means not waiting for certainty.

State estate taxes are a different matter

Twelve states and the District of Columbia impose their own estate tax, with exemptions often significantly lower than the federal threshold. Oregon and Massachusetts have a $1 million exemption — meaning estates above that threshold owe state estate tax even if they owe nothing federally. If you own real estate in multiple states, or if you live in a state with an estate tax, this affects your planning even if the federal estate tax never touches you.

What to actually do about this

For most families, the 2026 sunset changes nothing in the near term. A properly structured revocable trust, current beneficiary designations, and a clear plan for asset transfer are still the right foundation regardless of where the exemption lands.

For families with estates approaching $5 million — particularly those with illiquid assets like a family business or real estate — now is the time to have a conversation about what additional structures might make sense. Some planning options have time requirements and the window may be shorter than it appears.

For families well below these thresholds, the noise around the estate tax sunset is mostly noise. Your planning priorities are funding your trust, keeping beneficiary designations current, and making sure the documents you have still reflect your life as it actually is.
