The healthcare proxy nobody reads — until they need it.

Of all the documents in an estate plan, the healthcare proxy gets the least attention. It doesn't show up in probate. It doesn't affect asset distribution. And then one day it's the only document that matters.

Mia Smith, FRC, NSSA, FPWM · 6 min read · Octus Financial

Of all the documents in an estate plan, the healthcare proxy gets the least attention. It doesn't show up in probate. It doesn't affect how your assets are distributed. Most people sign it, put it in the binder, and never think about it again. And then, one day, it's the only document that matters.

A healthcare proxy — also called a healthcare power of attorney, medical power of attorney, or healthcare surrogate designation in Florida — names who can make medical decisions for you if you cannot make them yourself. Not after you die. During your lifetime, if you are incapacitated.

What 'springing' and 'immediate' actually mean

Most healthcare proxies are 'springing' — they only become effective when a physician (usually two, in most state statutes) certifies that you lack the capacity to make your own medical decisions. Until then, you make your own decisions. Some clients ask for an 'immediate' proxy — effective upon signing. This is less common and usually unnecessary. If you're asking because you want to reduce the documentation burden during a crisis, that's worth discussing.

The difference between a healthcare proxy and a living will

These are two different documents, often bundled together and confused with each other.

A healthcare proxy names a person — your agent, the human being who will speak for you.

A living will (also called an advance directive or directive to physicians) states your wishes directly — what interventions you do and don't want, under what circumstances, for what conditions. It speaks for you when your agent can't be reached, or when you want to relieve your agent of the burden of making a decision you've already made.

Having both is better than having one. The proxy handles the decision-making authority. The living will provides the instructions.

Who should you name?

The obvious answer is a spouse or adult child. The better answer is whoever is most likely to advocate calmly and clearly under stress, communicate with medical professionals without being dismissed, and honor your wishes even when those wishes are emotionally difficult.

That person is not always the same person who is best with finances. It is not always the oldest child. Name someone who will fight for what you actually want — not what makes them most comfortable.

The HIPAA release most people forget

Your healthcare agent cannot effectively advocate for you if they can't get information. HIPAA restricts what providers can share with anyone not named on your release. Your estate plan should include a HIPAA authorization that explicitly names the people who can receive your medical information. Without it, your healthcare agent may arrive at a hospital and be told, politely but firmly, that staff cannot confirm whether you're even a patient there.
